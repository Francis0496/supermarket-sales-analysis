Five data-driven decisions for the chain

A 3-branch supermarket chain, one quarter of point-of-sale data, distilled to the decisions worth acting on — each ranked by return, backed by its evidence, and filtered through statistical significance so we don't chase gaps that aren't real.

Period Jan–Mar 2019 (Q1) Branches A · Yangon  /  B · Mandalay  /  C · Naypyitaw Records 1,000 transactions (cleaned & recovered from 1,003) Margin fixed at 4.76%

The five decisions

Ordered by expected return on effort. Cards 1 and 3–5 are actions; card 2 is the decision not to act — equally valuable, because it redirects budget away from a phantom problem.

Deeper cuts — targeting & cross-sell

Two analyses the brief was asked for. Both are adaptations: this dataset has no customer ID (every invoice is unique) and one product line per invoice, so textbook RFM (needs repeat-visit history) and market-basket association rules (need multi-item baskets) are not computable. Below are the closest rigorous substitutes that answer the same questions — which customers are worth most and which category to cross-sell to whom — with the data limitation stated up front.

Confidence filter

Before committing money, every headline gap was tested at α = 0.05. This is the step that separates a decision brief from a dashboard of coincidences.

Method. Raw file de-duplicated (3 repeat invoices removed) and missing numerics recovered losslessly via the identity Total = Unit price × Quantity × 1.05 — 99.7% of rows retained rather than dropped. Figures are actuals from the cleaned dataset; dollar impacts are illustrative frames on stated assumptions (e.g. "+5% peak throughput"), not forecasts.
Caveat. One quarter (n = 1,000) is a strong operational read but too short to establish seasonality; treat weekday/hour patterns as current-state, not annualized. Significance tests use ANOVA, Welch's t-test, and χ² of independence.
